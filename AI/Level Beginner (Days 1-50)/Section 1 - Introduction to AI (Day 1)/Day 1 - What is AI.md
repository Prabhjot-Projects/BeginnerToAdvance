## 1. What is Artificial Intelligence?

### Definition

**Artificial Intelligence (AI)** is the science and engineering of creating machines and software that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence. These tasks include learning, reasoning, problem-solving, perception, and language understanding.

### Example

- Voice assistants like Siri or Alexa can understand spoken commands and respond.
- Recommendation systems on Netflix suggest movies based on your viewing habits.

## 2. Types of AI

### Definition

AI is commonly categorised into three types:

- **Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI):** AI designed for specific tasks. It excels at one thing but cannot generalise beyond it. Also called Weak AI.
- **Artificial General Intelligence (AGI):** Hypothetical AI that can perform any intellectual task a human can do, with flexible learning and reasoning.
- **Artificial Super Intelligence (ASI):** A future concept where AI surpasses human intelligence in all fields.

### Example

- **ANI:** Spam filters, image recognition software.
- **AGI:** Currently theoretical; no real-world examples yet.
- **ASI:** Speculative and futuristic AI systems.

## 3. Brief History of AI

### Definition

AI development began in the mid-20th century and has evolved through several key milestones:

- **1956:** The term “Artificial Intelligence” was coined at the Dartmouth Conference.
- **1960s–70s:** Symbolic AI and expert systems.
- **1997:** IBM’s Deep Blue defeated chess champion Garry Kasparov.
- **2010s:** Rapid advances in machine learning and deep learning enabled breakthroughs in speech, vision, and language processing.

### Example

- Deep Blue’s victory was a landmark that showed machines can outperform humans in complex tasks.

## 4. Simple AI Concept in Python (Conceptual Example)

### Definition

Basic AI often involves decision-making based on rules or data. This example uses a simple function to classify numbers.

### Example

```python
def classify_number(num):
    if num < 10:
        return "small"
    else:
        return "large"

print(classify_number(7))   # Output: small
print(classify_number(15))  # Output: large

```
